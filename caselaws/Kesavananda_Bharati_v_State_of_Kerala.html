Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)

Citation: AIR 1973 SC 1461

Facts:

The petitioner, Kesavananda Bharati, head of a math in Kerala, challenged the constitutional validity of the Kerala Land Reforms Act, 1963. During the pendency of his case, the Parliament passed the 24th, 25th, and 29th Constitutional Amendments, which the petitioner also challenged, arguing that they violated the fundamental rights under Article 14, 19, and 31.

Issue:

The core issue was whether the Parliament's power to amend the Constitution under Article 368 was absolute, or if there were inherent limitations. Specifically, could Parliament amend the "basic structure" of the Constitution?

Judgment:

The Supreme Court delivered its historic judgment by a razor-thin majority of 7-6. It held that while Parliament has the power to amend any part of the Constitution, it cannot alter its "basic structure." This doctrine, known as the **Basic Structure Doctrine**, places a limitation on the amending power and ensures that the Constitution's fundamental identity is not destroyed. The court ruled that fundamental rights are part of the basic structure.
